Mediterranean Journey

Athens, Greece

Mars Hill, the unknown God, and the gospel in the world's intellectual capital

Mediterranean Journey March 1, 2026 By Jeff Johnson Acts 17:22-23 - Acts 17:34

Biblical & Historical Background

Athens occupies a uniquely prominent place in Paul's missionary narrative as the intellectual and cultural capital of the ancient world. Acts 17:16 records that Paul's spirit was provoked within him as he saw that the city was full of idols. He reasoned in the synagogue and in the marketplace daily, drawing the attention of Epicurean and Stoic philosophers who brought him before the Areopagus — the ancient council that met on Mars Hill.

Paul's address on the Areopagus is a masterpiece of contextual apologetics. He begins with careful observation, noting the altar inscribed To the unknown god, then moves from their own poetic traditions — In him we live and move and have our being — to the proclamation of the one Creator God, the appointed day of judgment, and the resurrection of Jesus.

Gospel & Missions Work

Paul's method is instructive: he found genuine points of contact in the culture without compromising the gospel's distinctive claims. He did not soften the offense of the resurrection — the very point at which some in his audience mocked — because it was non-negotiable. Real conversions followed, including Dionysius the Areopagite and a woman named Damaris (Acts 17:34). The gospel divides and saves even in the most sophisticated intellectual settings.

Athens is one of the most active evangelical mission fields in Greece. The Greek Evangelical Church has its headquarters in Athens and maintains several congregations in the city. Perhaps the most remarkable recent development is the growth of gospel witness among Iranian, Afghan, and Syrian refugees — many proving extraordinarily receptive to the gospel — creating thriving new congregations that are reshaping the evangelical landscape. Paul stood on Mars Hill and declared the unknown God. That same proclamation is now reaching people from across the world who have arrived at Athens' shores.
