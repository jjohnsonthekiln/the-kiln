Gospel & Grace

Come to Me, All Who Labor

The yoke of Christ as rest, not burden

Gospel & Grace February 18, 2026 By Jeff Johnson Matthew 11:28-30

The invitation of Matthew 11:28 is the strangest paradox in the Gospels. Jesus calls the weary to take on a yoke. Rest is found not in the removal of burden but in the exchange of burdens — from the crushing weight of self-justification to the gentle load of grace.

First-century listeners heard yoke as a rabbinic metaphor for Torah observance and discipleship. Every teacher had a yoke — his interpretive school, his demands, his system. Jesus is not abolishing yokes; he is contrasting his yoke with every other. I am gentle and lowly in heart, he says. No rabbi before him ever described himself that way.

The weariness Jesus addresses is not primarily physical. It is the exhaustion of performing for an audience that is never satisfied. The yoke of Christ is a transfer of jurisdiction: from the court of human opinion to the verdict already spoken at the cross.

In counseling, we meet this weariness constantly. The anxious man who cannot rest because he cannot prove his worth. The perfectionistic woman who serves God as though grace had a co-pay. The gospel addresses not their behavior but their court. Christ has already spoken the verdict: Beloved.
